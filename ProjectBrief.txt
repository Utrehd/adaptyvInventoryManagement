Project: Inventory Management
Problem
Our lab runs automated protein characterization experiments at high throughput, and the quality of the data we produce depends on the lab running smoothly. Inventory management is part of the backbone of that system: every experiment consumes materials (DNA, proteins, buffers, consumables), and the lab cannot move faster than its ability to track what it has, what it needs, and where things are. 
Our production process consists of several steps, and each step has different requirements and some logic for how much of each component is used up. There are several types of materials that differ on how they are used and restocked, how fast they expire, and how much they can be reused across different projects.
The goal is to sketch out a system for tracking how much of each material we have at any given time, which processes use them, and where they are stored in the lab.
Use cases
Some questions the system should be able to answer:
Do I have enough materials to complete my lab tasks?
Some experiments require us to re-run some steps of the production process when the data generated don't pass QC. We need to know in advance if we have enough materials for these re-runs.
Binding experiments have Targets. These targets are expensive but can often be re-used across experiments. We need to know if we have enough left in stock from previous experiments or should order more.
Are these materials still viable / not expired?
Where are these materials stored in the lab?
As our lab operations grow in size, it will become harder and harder to find the right material for a given task.
Material types
 DNA
Specific to a project (sometimes reusable, but rare). DNA of the proteins that a customer wants to test against a target.
Stored in DNA plate wells (Twist Plate, Dilution Plate). Each well has a volume and a DNA concentration.
Twist Plate is ordered from a supplier. Takes 5-10 days to deliver. Expensive.
Dilution Plate is created from Twist Plate.
Lasts for many years in the freezer.
Stock created when DNA order (Twist Plate) received.
Consumed (partially) when moving from Dilution Plate to any other plate.
 Samples
Specific to a project. Proteins that a customer wants to test against a target.
Proteins expressed from the DNA sequences.
Stored in protein plate wells (Expression Plates, BLI Plates, GFP Plates). Each well has a volume and a protein concentration.
BLI Plates and GFP Plates are thrown away after they are used. Expression Plates last for 2 weeks.
Last for many years in the freezer.
Consumed (partially) when moving from Expression Plate to any other plate.
 Target proteins
Specific to a project, but different projects can share a target protein (a number of clinically relevant targets are tested by most customers).
A physical protein ordered from a supplier (already expressed). Takes 5-10 days to deliver. Expensive.
Lasts for many years in the freezer.
Stored in tubes (single well plate). Has a volume and concentration.
Volume and concentration are measured at the end of the target protein reconstitution step.
 Consumables
Plasticware used to handle samples. Includes pipette tips, well plates, chips, and other items.
Ordered from different suppliers. No expiration date. Consumed at different steps of the process.
 Buffers
Liquids in which we put proteins and DNA. Made in-house from components (that need to be ordered).
Expiration: GFP stock 6 months in the freezer, loading buffer 1 week, others 1 month.
Consumed at different steps of the process.
 Other materials
Buffer components, PBS, and so on.
Ordered from a supplier. Varying expiration dates, but typically long-lived.
Consumed at different steps of the process.
What we'd like to see
We want to see how you reason through a domain problem and translate it into the artifacts an engineer would actually produce. Please deliver two things.
A data model and SQL schema. Show us the entities you would create, their relationships, and the key columns. We care about how you handle the differences between material types, how you represent storage locations, how you track quantities and expiration, and how consumption is recorded. Note explicitly where you considered alternatives and what made you land on your choice.
An API design. The shape of the endpoints a frontend or another service would call to answer the use cases above. REST, RPC, GraphQL, whatever you prefer, but be specific about request and response shapes for the most important calls. We are interested in how you think about read patterns versus write patterns, and how you keep the surface coherent as the domain grows.
Scope and logistics
We give you 24 hours to work on this. We would much rather see depth on a few well-chosen pieces than breadth across everything, so if you cut things, tell us what you cut and why.
You are welcome to use AI tools freely. We care about the output and your reasoning, not how it was produced.
We will not be available to answer questions, so please bake any assumptions you need into your submission rather than waiting on clarification. The assumptions themselves are useful signal for us.
At the end of the 24 hours, you’ll present your approach to us and we’ll have a live discussion around it. Please send your work 1 hour before the scheduled meeting so we have time to go over it before the start.
